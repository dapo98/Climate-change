What is Climate Change?

To understand what climate change is, it is important to be able to distinguish what the difference is between climate and weather. Weather is the changes we feel everyday. One day it may be hot, another cold. One day it may rain, another it is sunny, this is what is defined as weather. Climate is the typical weather of a specific area. When describing the climate of the whole Earth the term, Earth's climate is typically used.

Now that the difference between weather and climate has been established, climate change can be discussed. Climate change is the change in weather over time in an area. Earth's climate change can be described as a change in the usual temperature in that typical period of time. For example, snow may fall in an area that is not prone to snow, or the opposite, snow does not fall in an area that it usually does.

What's the Issue?

Greenhouse gas emissions cause climate change by trapping heat and contribute to respiratory disease from smog and air pollution. Extreme weather, food supply disruptions, and increased wildfires are other effects of climate change caused by greenhouse gases. Greenhouse gas is emitted in a variety of harmful ways. In the city of New York the largest sources of these emissions are combustion of natural gas, use of electricity and combustion of gasoline, and generated energy used in the buildings.

Impact Now

The average temperature has risen some 2°F since 1970. Heat waves can cause heat-related illnesses, heat stroke, and other serious health problems. Heat waves also raise the likelihood that people who already have heart, lung, or other chronic conditions risk of getting sick. Warmer temperatures, which can expand ranges for disease-carrying insects, and can increase pollen production and air pollution. Pollen and pollution raise risks for people who suffer from asthma. Annual rain and snow has increased. Changing precipitation patterns such as drought and flooding may take the form of extreme events that directly cause human injury or death. Less extreme changes may affect public health in other ways, for example, by reducing the availability of water for drinking and other human needs, or by creating damp conditions in homes, schools, and workplaces that promote mold and other pests. Sea levels have risen significantly and will only continue in the same manner, by 2100 seas may rise 18 to 50 inches higher than today. Disruptions to agriculture from frequent drought, flooding, and unseasonal heat or frost events can interfere with successful food production.
